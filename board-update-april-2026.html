Church Partnership • South Kivu, DRC
A Community Ready for Transformation

One of our first activities since launching the new ECC project in South Kivu, DRC was to conduct a baseline survey to understand the current knowledge, attitudes, and practices around NTDs in the communities we serve. The survey captured responses from 708 participants, including religious leaders, community members, persons affected by NTDs, and healthcare staff.

The results validate the project's church-based model and are encouraging for what's to come. The community is ready for this approach: 96.3% of all respondents want faith-health integrated training, and 100% of healthcare workers — every single one surveyed — support structured collaboration with religious leaders, making it one of the strongest findings in the entire survey.

The foundation for transformation is there; our project is providing the structure to make it happen.

We also found that over half of pastors feel unequipped to discuss NTDs despite having formal theological training, which tells us the project's training modules are filling a critical gap. The survey confirmed that stigma remains a real barrier, with 60% of persons affected reporting it delayed their access to care — but it also revealed that 81% of community members already believe the church should be part of the solution.

These baseline findings will shape our training curricula and implementation priorities, and will serve as the benchmarks against which we measure impact as the project progresses. Additional insights from the survey will be presented at the May board meeting.

From the Field
Updates Across the Mission
Global • Leprosy Coalition

ILEP Restructuring

The International Federation of Anti-Leprosy Associations (ILEP) is undergoing significant restructuring. The Global Partnership for Zero Leprosy will conclude by the end of the calendar year, and Novartis is expected to join ILEP directly, preserving the multi-stakeholder collaboration that the partnership fostered. ILEP will adopt a smaller operational model with a general secretary role replacing the current CEO structure. Constitutional changes will be formalized in June, with the new structure taking effect July 1. The restructuring reflects revenue pressures faced across the global leprosy sector.

Research • Vaccine Development

LepVax Update

The GHIT Fund grant supporting Phase 1B of the LepVax leprosy vaccine trial has been terminated, placing the project on pause while the team regroups to assess remaining funding and coordinate with research partners. Manufacturing of new vaccine batches, the most recent milestone, is on hold pending a confirmed trial start date. Becky Rivoire continues to lead the coordination effort.

The loss of the GHIT grant will put pressure on our ability to ensure our internal program ratio meets policy. So far, we are managing this challenge, and we will provide a further update at the May board meeting.

Asia • Conference

CCIH Regional Meeting — Philippines

Jim and Christopher will travel to the Philippines the week of April 20 for the CCIH 2026 Regional Meeting, themed "Advancing Health and Hope in a Changing World." The conference provides an important platform for strengthening partnerships across the Asia region. We look forward to sharing outcomes from that engagement at the May staff meeting.

Research • Spring Meetings

Leprosy Research Initiative Presentations

Three HRI-supported research projects will be presented at the LRI spring meetings (virtual this year): a handheld ultrasound study for leprosy diagnosis with partners in India and Nepal, the LepVax vaccine trial, and a project in Ghana engaging persons affected as peer mentors alongside the national leprosy control program.

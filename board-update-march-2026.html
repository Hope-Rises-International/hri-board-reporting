The Church in Action • Upper East Region, Ghana
A Blind Pastor, 50 Patients, and the Power of a Story Shared

Pastor James cannot see the faces of the people he serves. But they see him—and that has made all the difference.

In Ghana’s Upper East Region, our team partnered with the Help Foundation and Eastwood Anaba Church to address hydrocele, a painful complication of lymphatic filariasis that carries deep stigma in rural communities. The strategy was simple and familiar to us: train pastors and church leaders to mobilize patients, walk with them to the hospital, and pray with them before and after surgery.

Pastor James became the most effective mobilizer of all. Blind since childhood, he had a hydrocelectomy himself at age 14. He used that experience—his own vulnerability, his own healing—to reach men who were afraid to seek care. He convinced over 50 suspected patients to come forward. He prayed with them at the hospital. He turned his wound into a bridge.

This is the Core Approach at work. Not a program that happens to involve a church, but a church that becomes the program. Without Pastor James, without the Eastwood Anaba Church, this work does not happen. The church is not the venue. The church is the engine.

Our staff are now exploring how these same trained pastors might address a Yaws outbreak and leprosy cases emerging in the area—the church infrastructure, once built, extends far beyond a single disease.

Christopher Baskeran Joins as Church Engagement Lead

Christopher joined Hope Rises on February 16 and immediately traveled with Jim Oehrig to India and Sri Lanka. His expertise in church partnerships, integral mission, and community health makes him an ideal fit for building our Asia portfolio—where two projects are confirmed for FY27, with several additional opportunities being evaluated for strategic fit with our Core Approach.

Sri Lanka • Partnership Development

Alliance Development Trust: Churches Confronting a Leprosy Surge

Sri Lanka is seeing a troubling surge in leprosy cases—and the numbers carry a particular weight: ten percent of those diagnosed are children under 14. Jim and Christopher met with Dr. Indira Kahawita, Consultant Dermatologist and head of the national Anti Leprosy Campaign, strengthening our connection with government health leadership at a critical moment.

Our partner ADT is engaging local churches and the national leprosy control program to address stigma and connect patients to care. Importantly, ADT has also identified significant burden from lymphatic filariasis and cutaneous leishmaniasis—presenting an opportunity to address multiple NTDs through the same church networks without significant additional cost. A potential Gift-in-Kind shipment through the Methodist hospital network is being explored for early FY27.

Renate was scheduled for reconstructive surgery in South Africa—a significant step in her recovery. Her visa was denied by the South African embassy and has been resubmitted. Fundraising
Easter: A Season of Restoration
Luntadila, featured in this year’s Easter campaign

Easter is one of our largest fundraising seasons. This year’s multi-channel campaign features Luntadila, a young woman from DR Congo whose leprosy was identified by a pastor trained through our program, leading to treatment and the restoration of her livelihood. The campaign is coordinated across direct mail, email, and social media.

At the same time, our major donor team is focused on cultivation and gratitude this season—handwritten Easter cards are going to all portfolio donors and those who have included us in their estate plans. Over the past year, testing has revealed clear patterns in how different donors engage, and a hybrid mailing plan is in place for the fiscal year-end push.

Feature: Innovation & Stewardship
Building Smarter, Not Bigger

Led by CEO Bill Simmons as the primary builder using AI-powered development tools, our team has deployed 15 intelligent systems—directed in plain English, without hiring developers or purchasing new software. These tools automate work that used to consume hundreds of staff hours every month: sorting email, generating tax receipts, tracking timesheets, scoring campaigns, and giving our fundraising team real-time visibility into donor relationships.

This is not about reducing headcount. It is about freeing our people to do the work that only they can do: building relationships, making strategic decisions, and serving the communities we exist to reach.
